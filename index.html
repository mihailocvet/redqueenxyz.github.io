Intro

A modern approach to data.

There is a consistent and pressing demand for intelligent technologies in multiple verticals of our economy, and this demand is turning into an arms race. Large consulting groups, high-tech firms, advertising agencies, and a myriad of other participants are competing to hire and arm the best talent in the industry, in order to be ahead of their competitors, and prepare for the inevitable AI-assisted future.

Unfortunately, not only is such a talent pool incredibly small for the demand, the division and disconnection of these people and their tools means that most data science teams are isolated, and are forced to reinvent the wheel in their own particular niche in the industry, often rediscovering the same methodologies using the same tools.

In the short-term, firms are hiring independent or small teams being forced to tackle problems of incredible complexity without the ability to effectively communicate their difficulties (or results) to the people they are meant to inform, and are forced to consistently reinvent the practice of data science for their particular agency (which while occasionally unique, is rarely meaningful differentiable at the executional level.)

In the long-term, thisf orces each interested firm to develop an understanding of this technology can be implemented locally, how to hire the right people to solve their unique problem, and potentially risk hundreds of thousands of dollars investing in tools and talent that may or may not solve their problem.

This problem is endemic of the old way we've approached data and analytics. Protected, sileoed, private, and internalized. This approach, while fundamentally secure and potentially necessary for truly revolutionary technologies, forces any interested participant in the industry to compete with each other Machines have the potential to change the way work, live, and survive as a species.

About

"Here, it takes all the running you can do, just to keep in the same place. If you want to get somewhere else, you must run at least twice as fast as that!"
- Red Queen (Lewis Carroll), Alice in Wonderland

The Red Queen Hypothesis, is an evolutionary hypothesis which proposes that organisms must constantly adapt, evolve, and proliferate not just to gain reproductive advantage, but to simply to survive against other ever-evolving organisms in an ever-changing environment.

The basis for the theory can be summarized as ‘the evolutionary arms race’, where prey and predator must constantly evolve together to reach any sort of balance. One could liken this arms race to the data arms race happening across economies around the world, in what is being terms the Fourth Idustrial Revolution .


"The First Industrial Revolution used steam power to mechanize production. The Second used electric power to create mass production. The Third used electronics and information technology to automate production.

Now a Fourth Industrial Revolution is building on the Third. It is...[the] fusion of technologies blurring the lines between the physical, digital, and biological."
- Professor Klaus Schwab

It has become increasingly clear that our technologies have fundamentally changed the environment in which we do business, and it will only be the firms that are prepared to evolve to embrace our new tools that will survive this revolution.

We at Red Queen anticipate this transition, by building the next generation of Intelligent Technologies that can be integrated, leveraged, and deployed by firms to position them to survive this race and bring smarter goods and services to the economies of the future.
We hope to improve our skills and offering in order to bring the very latest tools to our species, and by advancing our cause through the use of these Intelligent Technologies.
